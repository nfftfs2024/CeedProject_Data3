Introduction

You might know the importance of 'good working posture' and that too much sitting is unhealthy, but do you know how to set up a computer workstation to reduce the chance of sprain and strain injuries? Office ergonomics can help staff be more comfortable at work, lower stress and reduced injuries caused by awkward postures and repetitive tasks.

This Information Page explains steps to setting up a computer work station and using a computer for all Data#3 work locations - our offices, customer or vendor site, home based and travelling for work (mobile workers). It also provides a link to information for taller workers.

Responsibilities

  1. Business units and work areas are responsible for managing risks associated with computers and ergonomics and ensuring a good work station set up for workers for all work locations.
  2. Managers and supervisors are responsible for ensuring computer use in their area is managed, computer equipment is well maintained, staff complete the online Office Ergonomics Training and that office based staff complete the Workstation Self-Assessment Checklist and home based staff complete the Home Working WHS and Work Station Assessment. Concerns must be managed and referral to HSE if unresolved. 
  3. Personnel who use a computer are responsible for undertaking the online  Office Ergonomics Training and following directions on health and safety and computer use.  Staff who are office based must complete a Workstation Self-Assessment Checklist when they start their employment and annually thereafter, and earlier if they identify concerns with their work station set up or changes at work that impact on computer use. Staff who are home based must complete the Home Working WHS and Work Station Assessment. Concerns must be reported to their Manager or Supervisor. An Incident Report Form and BCS should be logged if health concerns are experienced. 

Office Ergonomics 

The information below provides general directions for a comfortable work station setup and managing health and safety risks with computers. 

Desk

  • Desks should be standard height unless workers are tall (>6'3") when a raised height or height adjustable desk may be needed (refer to Office Ergonomics for Tall People for information). 
  • Height adjustable/sit stand desks are available in Data#3 offices for staff who are tall, require a raised height desk for medical or health reasons and/or prefer to vary their posture during work (refer to Sit-Stand Work Stations for information).

Chair

  • Chairs should provide height and back adjustment and lumbar support, users should also adjust the chair to suit their needs.
  • Sit close to the desk and use a chair without armrests if needed to achieve this.
  • Feet should be flat on the floor; foot stools may be used to provide foot and leg support and promote a good posture.
  • Raised height chairs and sit/stand stools may be required for workers who are tall or require a raised height chair.

Screen

  • Screens should be free of glare and shadowing and large enough to view work.
  • Top of the screen casing should be approximately eye level and screen characters clearly seen.
  • Where glasses or contact lenses are worn these should suit computer use.

Other Equipment

  • Document holders are available and should be used where needed.
  • If using a laptop for >2 hours/day, a hot desk or mobile worker (working in different locations), use a separate keyboard, mouse and monitor or lap top riser (refer to Office Ergonomics for Mobile Workers for information).
  • Computer equipment that is used regularly (mouse, keyboard etc.) should be close to the worker on the desk, to minimise the need to stretch or reach out the hands or arms for use.

Home based workers are required to provide their own work station equipment suitable for computer use and adhere to ergonomic guidelines provided.

Work Practices

  • Computer users should take the time to ensure they have a comfortable posture, as a poor set up can strain the arms, neck, hands, eyes and back.
  • Workers are encouraged to use good keying practices when using the keyboard and not type or hit the keys too vigorously
  • Workers should take regular and sufficient breaks from computer use to stretch and rest. Rests may be micro-breaks (a few seconds every few minutes), mini breaks (a fewminutes every hour) and longer breaks (i.e. during morning tea/lunch/attending meetings). Rests may be a change of work practices, such as going for a meeting, to the printer or lunch break.

Training and Information

Workers who use a computer for work should complete the online Office Ergonomics Training course when they start work and annually during employment, or sooner if they would like to remind themselves on health and safety and computer use.

Musculo-Skeletal Symptoms

Mobile workers should report any musculo-skeletal symptoms they experience when using their computer. Musculo-skeletal symptoms are best treated promptly by identifying and restricting activities that may have caused or aggravate the symptoms, rest and, if needed, a treatment plan directed by their GP..

Where concerns are raised, the following actions should be taken:

  • Notify their Manager or Supervisor, complete a Workstation Self-Assessment Checklist and make improvements to their work area and posture.
  • If symptoms are unresolved, worker should see their GP for an examination and advice on ways to resolve symptoms.
  • Refer to their HR Advisor and/or HSE by logging a BCS for the attention of WHS.

When HSE are notified, they may undertake an assessment to understand the cause of the symptoms and agree actions to address the concerns raised.
